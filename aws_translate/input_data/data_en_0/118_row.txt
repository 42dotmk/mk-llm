The genetic code is the set of rules that the cell's machinery follows to convert or translate the information encoded in the nucleotide sequence of mRNA into a corresponding amino acid sequence for protein synthesis. This code consists of a triplet code, where three nucleotides, called codons, represent one amino acid. In total, there are 64 possible codons, which code for 20 different amino acids and 3 stop signals.

The process of translation can be mainly divided into three stages: initiation, elongation, and termination. Here's a detailed explanation of each stage:

1. Initiation: The process begins with the formation of the initiation complex, consisting of the small ribosomal subunit, an initiator tRNA molecule carrying the first amino acid (usually methionine) and an mRNA molecule. The small ribosomal subunit binds to the mRNA, and the initiator tRNA binds to the mRNA's start codon (AUG).

2. Elongation: In this phase, the large ribosomal subunit attaches to the small subunit, forming a complete ribosome complex. The codons in the mRNA are read sequentially by the ribosome, attracting corresponding tRNA molecules carrying specific amino acids. The amino acids are then linked through peptide bonds, gradually creating the growing polypeptide chain.

3. Termination: The process of translation ends when the ribosome encounters a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA) on the mRNA. This signals the termination, and the newly synthesized polypeptide chain is released from the tRNA and ribosome. The ribosome dissociates into its subunits, and the mRNA is released for potential re-use or degradation.

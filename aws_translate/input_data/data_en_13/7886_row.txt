1. Ariane 5:

Ariane 5 is a European expendable launch system designed and developed by the European Space Agency (ESA) and the Ariane Group, a joint venture of CNES and Airbus Defence and Space. It is the fifth generation of the Ariane rocket family and has been in service since 1996.

The primary goals behind the development of Ariane 5 were to deliver increased payload capacity, improved reliability and cost efficiency, and be compatible with a broader range of missions. Ariane 5 is primarily used for launching communication satellites into geostationary transfer orbits (GTO), but is also capable of launching payloads to low Earth orbit (LEO), medium Earth orbit (MEO), polar orbits, and interplanetary missions.

The Ariane 5 has two primary configurations:
  a. Ariane 5 ECA (Evolution Cryotechnique type A): This is the most powerful version of Ariane 5, with a payload capacity of up to 10.5 metric tons to GTO.
  b. Ariane 5 ES (Evolution Storable): This version is designed for various types of missions and has a slightly lower GTO payload capacity of up to 8 metric tons.

2. Technical Specifications:

The Ariane 5 rocket is a two-stage vehicle, utilizing a cryogenic liquid hydrogen (LH2) and liquid oxygen (LOX) main stage referred to as the EPC (Étage Principal Cryotechnique) and a solid rocket booster stage.

- Main Stage: The EPC is equipped with the updated Vulcain 2 rocket engine. The main stage has a height of 30.5 meters and a diameter of 5.4 meters. It carries approximately 175 metric tons of LH2 and LOX as fuel.

- Solid Rocket Boosters: Ariane 5 employs two solid rocket boosters (EAP - Étages d'Accélération à Poudre) that are connected to the EPC. Each booster is approximately 31.6 meters in height with a diameter of 3.06 meters.

The combination of EPC and the two solid rocket boosters delivers efficient lift-off and stage separation, allowing for the transportation of heavy payloads into orbit.

3. Launch Site: ELA-3

Ensemble de Lancement Ariane 3, or ELA-3, is a dedicated launch facility for the Ariane 5 rocket located at the Guiana Space Center in French Guiana, near Kourou. The Guiana Space Center was chosen due to its proximity to the equator, which provides a performance advantage during launch, impeccable safety measures, and seclusion. ELA-3 was specifically designed and built for Ariane 5 operations starting in the 1990s.

ELA-3 comprises several key elements for a successful Ariane 5 launch:
  a. The Launch Control Center (LCC): This houses the control systems and technical teams that oversee all pre-launch activities for the Ariane 5, from assembly to countdown sequences.
  b. Launcher Integration Building (BIL): The first stage of the integration process occurs here, including assembly of the main stage and the attachment of solid boosters.
  c. Final Assembly Building (BAF): The payload, spacecraft, and final integration occur in this building, after which the Ariane 5 is moved to the launch zone.
  d. The Launch Zone (ZL): This is the actual launch site, where the fully integrated Ariane 5 rocket is prepared for liftoff.

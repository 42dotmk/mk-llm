The Lacey Act of 1900, named after Congressman John F. Lacey of Iowa, was the first federal law in the United States aimed at regulating commercial animal markets and protecting wildlife. It was enacted to preserve the country's natural resources by curbing the illegal trafficking of wild animals and plants, which also helped maintain ecological balance throughout the country. The act has been amended several times since its inception to expand its scope and cover a broader range of species, as well as plants.

1. Prohibition of interstate trade in wildlife and plants, taken, possessed, transported, or sold in violation of state, federal, tribal, or foreign laws: The act forbids the sale and transport of wildlife and plants deemed illegal under existing laws, thereby addressing the issue of wildlife and plant trafficking across state and national borders.

The introduction of non-native species can pose significant threats to native ecosystems, as it can disrupt delicate food chains and cause the decline of native species populations. This provision aims to minimize the risk of such introductions.

3. Regulation of commercial animal markets: The act regulates the commercial trade of protected wildlife by requiring businesses and individuals dealing with certain protected species to obtain permits. This helps to ensure that only legally obtained animals and plants are commercially traded.

4. Penalties: Violations of the Lacey Act can result in substantial civil and criminal penalties. The severity of these penalties depends on the nature of the offense, the value of the wildlife or plant involved, and whether the violator is operating as an individual or a corporation. Additionally, the act allows for the seizure of any illegally obtained wildlife, plants, or their products, as well as the equipment and vehicles used in their trafficking.

5. International cooperation: The Lacey Act covers not only violations of U.S. federal and state laws, but also violations of foreign laws that protect wildlife and plants. This allows for better international coordination in combating wildlife and plant trafficking.

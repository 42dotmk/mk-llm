The Poona Pact is a historical agreement that took place on 24th September 1932 between prominent Indian leaders Dr. Babasaheb Ambedkar and Mahatma Gandhi. The Pact was a significant event in the Indian political landscape of the 1930s when the British colonial government was devising a plan to give various sections of Indian society political representation.

Dr. Ambedkar, a leader of the Dalit community, was advocating for separate electorates for the untouchables or depressed classes in India. On the other hand, Mahatma Gandhi opposed the idea of separate electorates, fearing that it would further divide Indian society along caste lines and hinder the country's progress towards independence. To mitigate the situation, Gandhi went on a fast unto death in the Yerwada Central Jail, Pune, to urge reconsideration.

As a result, the Poona Pact was signed, which brought about a compromise between the two leaders. Instead of separate electorates, the Pact proposed reserved seats for the depressed classes within the general electorate based on their population. The agreement led to significant representation for the marginalized sections in the provincial and central legislative councils.

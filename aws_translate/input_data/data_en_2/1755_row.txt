Frederick Douglass was a former enslaved African American, who became a prominent abolitionist, orator, writer, and statesman in the 19th century. Below, you'll find a list of his significant achievements:

1. Escaping Slavery: At the age of 20, Douglass courageously escaped from slavery in Maryland to freedom in the North. This was his initial step towards becoming a renowned leader in the fight against slavery.

2. Authoring Autobiographies: Throughout his lifetime, Douglass penned several autobiographies detailing his experiences as a slave and his journey towards emancipation. His first autobiography, "Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave" (1845), became an instant bestseller and was followed by "My Bondage and My Freedom" (1855) and "Life and Times of Frederick Douglass" (1881).

3. Abolitionist Speaker: As an eloquent orator, Douglass began advocating for abolition in earnest in the 1840s. He would attend anti-slavery meetings and share his personal experiences as a slave, raising awareness about the cruelties of the institution and inspiring others to fight for its eradication.

4. Founding and Editing Newspapers: Douglass founded and served as the editor of several influential abolitionist newspapers, such as The North Star (1847-51), Frederick Douglass' Paper (1851-60), and New National Era (1870-74). These newspapers not only helped disseminate abolitionist ideas, but also promoted equal rights for African Americans and women.

5. Women's Suffrage Supporter: Douglass was a fervent supporter of the women's suffrage movement and contributed to the cause by attending conventions, writing about the issue, and participating in debates. He was one of the few male attendees at the historic Seneca Falls Convention in 1848, where he supported the proclamation of women's right to vote.

6. Recruiting Union Soldiers: During the American Civil War, Douglass played an active role in recruiting African American soldiers for the Union Army. He helped form the famous 54th Massachusetts Infantry Regiment, one of the first all-black regiments in the United States.

7. Political Appointments: Frederick Douglass held several significant political positions after the Civil War, including:
   - Presidential Elector in the 1872 election
   - President of the Freedman's Savings Bank (1874)
   - United States Marshal for the District of Columbia (1877-1881)
   - U.S. Recorder of Deeds for the District of Columbia (1881-1886)
   - U.S. Minister to Haiti (1889-1891)

8. Civil Rights Advocate: Douglass fought for the civil rights of African Americans throughout his life, campaigning for the passage of the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments, which abolished slavery, granted African Americans citizenship, and provided them with the right to vote, respectively.

In summary, Frederick Douglass's achievements consist of his escape from slavery, contributions to the abolitionist movement, support of women's rights, involvement in the Civil War, and participation in various political roles. As a writer, speaker, and activist, he left an indelible mark on American history and continues to be celebrated for his invaluable efforts toward social change.

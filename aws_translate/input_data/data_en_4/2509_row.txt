The Gram stain is a method used in microbiology to differentiate bacterial species into two groups based on their cell wall composition; Gram-positive and Gram-negative. However, it is important to note that the Gram stain is not the ideal method for diagnosing tuberculosis. The causative agent of tuberculosis is Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which is an acid-fast bacillus and does not stain well using the Gram stain method due to the presence of unique lipids and a waxy coat. Instead, a technique called the acid-fast stain (Ziehl-Neelsen stain or Kinyoun stain) is used to identify Mycobacterium tuberculosis.

Despite the limitations of the Gram stain method for diagnosing tuberculosis, I will explain the Gram staining procedure in general. The steps of the Gram stain technique include:

1. Collection of sample: Sputum or other body fluid samples are collected from the patient and placed on a glass slide.

2. Heat fixation: The sample on the slide is heat-fixed by quickly passing it over a flame. This helps to adhere the bacteria to the slide and kills them without causing significant distortion of their cellular structure.

3. Crystal violet: The slide is then flooded with a basic purple dye called crystal violet for 1 minute, which stains all bacterial cells on the slide.

4. Iodine treatment: A mordant, Gram's iodine, is added to the slide, which forms a complex with the crystal violet, adhering the dye to the bacterial cell walls.

5. Decolorization: The slide is then washed using a decolorizing agent, commonly an alcohol-acetone solution. At this stage, the Gram-negative bacteria lose the crystal violet-iodine complex due to the dissolution of their outer membrane. Gram-positive bacteria retain the complex due to the presence of thick peptidoglycan layer in their cell walls.

6. Counterstain: The slide is then flooded with a secondary dye, safranin, for 1-2 minutes. Safranin stains the decolorized Gram-negative bacteria with a pink to red color, while the Gram-positive bacteria remain violet.

7. Microscopy: The slide is finally rinsed, dried and examined under a microscope. Based on the color of the bacteria, they can be classified as Gram-positive (purple) or Gram-negative (pink).

The primary types of taxes in the United States include:

1. Income Tax: This is levied on individuals, corporations, estates, and trusts in the United States. The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) is the federal agency responsible for administering tax laws and collecting federal income taxes. U.S. citizens and residents are subject to federal income tax on their worldwide income regardless of where they live. Additionally, most states, including Illinois, impose a state income tax on their residents.

2. Payroll Tax: Employers and employees contribute to Social Security and Medicare through payroll taxes. These taxes are used to fund the Social Security program, which provides retirement, survivor, and disability benefits, as well as the Medicare program, which provides health insurance for older adults and certain individuals with disabilities. Both the employer and the employee contribute an equal portion of these taxes based on the employee's earnings.

3. Sales Tax: This is a consumption-based tax imposed on the sale of goods and services and is collected at the point of sale, typically at the state and local government levels. Sales tax rates and the range of taxable items vary from state to state and even within local jurisdictions.

4. Property Tax: Property owners pay taxes on the value of their real estate and, in some cases, personal property (such as vehicles, boats, and airplanes). Property taxes are usually assessed and collected by local governments, like counties and municipalities, and fund local services, such as public education, infrastructure maintenance, and law enforcement.

5. Excise Tax: This is a tax levied on specific goods, services, and activities, such as gasoline, cigarettes, alcohol, and air travel. Excise taxes are imposed by federal, state, and local governments and are meant to discourage or penalize certain types of consumption or behavior, or raise revenue for specific purposes, such as highway construction.

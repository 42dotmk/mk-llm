The practices of Buddhism are based on the teachings of Siddhartha Gautama, also known as the Buddha. Buddhism emphasizes the cultivation of wisdom, ethical conduct, and mental discipline to achieve the ultimate goal of enlightenment, or Nirvana. There are several core teachings and practices central to Buddhism, including the Four Noble Truths, the Eightfold Path, the Five Precepts, and meditation.

1. The Four Noble Truths: The Four Noble Truths are the foundational teachings of Buddhism and serve as a guide to understanding the nature of existence. They are:

   a) The truth of suffering (Dukkha): Life is characterized by suffering, be it physical, emotional, or mental.

   b) The truth of the cause of suffering (Samudaya): Suffering is caused by craving and attachment to transient objects and experiences. This cause is often referred to as the Three Poisonsâ€”desire, hatred, and ignorance.

   c) The truth of the end of suffering (Nirodha): By eliminating craving and attachment, one can achieve the cessation of suffering.

   d) The truth of the path to the cessation of suffering (Magga): The path to the cessation of suffering is found through following the Eightfold Path.

2. The Eightfold Path: This path offers practical guidance and practice for achieving the cessation of suffering. The Eightfold Path consists of:

   a) Right Understanding: Developing a deep understanding of the Four Noble Truths.

   b) Right Intention: Cultivating an attitude of compassionate non-harming and renouncing craving.

   c) Right Speech: Communicating truthfully, kindly, and helpfully.

   d) Right Action: Acting ethically and responsibly toward oneself and others.

   e) Right Livelihood: Engaging in an occupation that promotes peace and well-being, avoiding those that contribute to suffering.

   f) Right Effort: Diligently working to maintain wholesome states of mind and eliminate unwholesome ones.

   g) Right Mindfulness: Developing awareness and being fully present in each moment.

   h) Right Concentration: Practicing meditation to cultivate deeper states of concentration and insight.

3. The Five Precepts: These precepts serve as guidelines for ethical conduct in daily life. They are:

   a) Abstaining from taking life.
   
   b) Abstaining from taking what is not freely given.
   
   c) Abstaining from sexual misconduct.
   
   d) Abstaining from false speech.
   
   e) Abstaining from intoxicants leading to heedlessness.

4. Meditation: Meditation is a central practice in Buddhism for cultivating mindfulness, concentration, and insight. There are many forms of meditation in Buddhism, with two primary types being:

   a) Mindfulness meditation (Vipassana): A practice focused on developing insight into the nature of reality through observation of the impermanence, suffering, and selflessness of phenomena.

   b) Concentration meditation (Samatha): A practice aimed at developing deep concentration and mental tranquility, often by focusing on a single object or concept.

By engaging in these practices, Buddhists aim to eradicate ignorance and cultivate wisdom, fostering direct understanding and perception of dependent origination (the interconnected nature of phenomena), and ultimately, achieving enlightenment.

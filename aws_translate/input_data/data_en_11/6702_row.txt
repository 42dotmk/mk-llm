Photosynthesis is a vital process that occurs in plants, algae, and some bacteria, enabling these organisms to create food and energy from sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide. The overall process can be broken down into several stages and steps, as follows:

1. Absorption of water and nutrients from the soil by the plant roots: This is the missing first step in the given process. Plant roots are responsible for absorbing water and essential minerals, such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium, from the soil. Root hairs are specialized structures that increase the surface area in contact with the soil, enhancing absorption capabilities. The absorbed water and nutrients are then transported to other parts of the plant, including the leaves, through specialized tissues called xylem.

2. Water is transported to the leaves in the xylem: Water and nutrients absorbed by the roots are transported to the upper parts of the plant through the xylem. This upward movement is facilitated by processes such as capillary action, root pressure, and transpiration pull, ensuring that water reaches the leaves where it will be utilized in photosynthesis.

3. Energy is harvested through the light reaction: In the chlorophyll-containing cells of the plant leaves, light energy is absorbed and converted into chemical energy through a series of complex reactions known as the light-dependent reactions, or light reactions. Here, light energy is used to split water molecules into oxygen (O2), protons (H+), and electrons. This process generates oxygen and high energy compounds such as ATP (adenosine triphosphate) and NADPH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate), which will be used to fuel other reactions in the plant.

4. Carbon dioxide and energy from ATP are used to create sugar: After the light reactions, the plant moves to the next stage of photosynthesis known as the Calvin cycle or light-independent reactions. Here, carbon dioxide is combined with the energy from ATP and the electrons from NADPH to form glucose and other organic molecules, which serve as the building blocks for various cellular structures and functions.

5. Oxygen exits the leaves through the stomata by diffusion: The oxygen produced during the light reaction is no longer needed by the plant and is released into the atmosphere through specialized openings called stomata found on the leaf's surface. The stomata facilitate gas exchange by permitting the entry of carbon dioxide and the exit of oxygen and water vapor.

6. The plant reuses the water or the water exits through the stomata as water vapor: The water produced during the photosynthesis process can be reused by the plant as needed, or it can be released back into the atmosphere through the process of transpiration. This release of water vapor through the stomata aids in maintaining a proper balance of water and minerals within the plant, as well as regulating temperature and pulling more water up from the roots.
